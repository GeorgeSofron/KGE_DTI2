1. OVERALL RANKING: ComplEx > TriModel > TransE
-----------------------------------------------
ComplEx is the clear best performer across all dimensions and metrics. At dim=300
it achieves AUC-ROC of 0.799 and AUC-PR of 0.479 — roughly +13% AUC-ROC and
+21% AUC-PR above TransE at the same dimension. This is consistent with
expectations: DTI relationships are inherently asymmetric (a drug targets a
protein, not vice versa), and ComplEx's Hermitian dot product is specifically
designed to model asymmetric relations, giving it a structural advantage over
TransE's translation assumption.

TriModel sits in the middle, performing comparably to ComplEx at dim=300
(AUC-ROC: 0.796 vs 0.799) but lagging significantly at lower dimensions. This
suggests TriModel needs higher capacity to generalise, whereas ComplEx captures
DTI structure efficiently even at smaller embeddings.

TransE is the weakest across all configurations. Its distance-based symmetric
scoring is poorly suited for directed drug-target relations, producing AUC-PR
values as low as 0.174 at dim=100 — barely above the random baseline of ~0.087
(prevalence = 2894/33203 ≈ 8.7%).


2. EFFECT OF EMBEDDING DIMENSION
---------------------------------
All three models improve consistently as dimension increases from 100 → 200 → 300:

  Model       AUC-PR gain (100→300)
  ----------  ---------------------
  TransE      +0.098  (+56%)
  ComplEx     +0.075  (+19%)
  TriModel    +0.147  (+57%)

TransE and TriModel benefit most from larger dimensions — they appear
capacity-starved at dim=100. ComplEx gains less because its complex-valued
representations are already efficient at capturing relational structure.
Importantly, no model shows signs of overfitting as dim increases; performance
monotonically improves.


3. PRECISION-RECALL TRADE-OFF
------------------------------
At the best F1 threshold:

- ComplEx (dim=300) achieves the best balance: Precision=0.558 / Recall=0.451.
  It favours precision, consistent with a conservative ranker.

- TriModel (dim=200/300) leans toward recall (Recall=0.440–0.449 vs
  Precision=0.342–0.418), useful when the goal is maximising candidate retrieval
  (e.g., drug repurposing screens).

- TransE has consistently low precision (0.189–0.326) meaning a large fraction
  of its positive predictions are false positives — unreliable for clinical
  prioritisation.


4. AUC-PR vs AUC-ROC GAP
--------------------------
AUC-PR is substantially lower than AUC-ROC for all models, which is expected
given the 10:1 class imbalance — AUC-ROC can be inflated by the large pool of
true negatives. AUC-PR is the more honest indicator here. Even ComplEx's best
AUC-PR of 0.479 means there is substantial room for improvement, suggesting
that KGE plausibility scores alone are a useful but incomplete signal for DTI
prediction.


5. KEY TAKEAWAYS
----------------
- Use ComplEx (dim=300) for the best DTI predictions from this framework.

- Dimension matters, especially for TriModel — consider testing dim=400+ to see
  if the trend continues.

- TransE should not be used for DTI prediction in this KG without modification;
  its symmetric scoring function is a fundamental mismatch for the task.

- The 603 OOV drops (same for all models since they share the same KG
  vocabulary) represent ~17% of test positives — worth investigating whether
  these are systematically harder/easier pairs.
